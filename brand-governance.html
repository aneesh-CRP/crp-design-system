Using the CRP brand, with care.

This page exists so you don't have to guess. If you're making something with the CRP logo, colors, or voice on it — internal or external — start here.

Why we have a brand system

Three principles behind every rule on this page.

CRP talks to people at vulnerable moments — asking them to trust us with their bodies and time. The brand is how we earn that trust before a word is said.

01

Consistency earns trust

Participants see dozens of "research studies" ads a week. The ones that look the same in the email, on the flyer, and in the clinic are the ones they show up for. Inconsistency reads as amateur — and amateur reads as risky.

02

Warmth over polish

We're not a pharma giant. The Market Pro script, the orange hand-drawn accents, the plain-English voice — these are deliberate signals that a human reads your chart, not a database.

03

Accessibility is non-negotiable

Our audience skews older, sometimes low-vision, sometimes non-native English. 14.8:1 navy-on-white, 44px hit targets, translated consent forms. WCAG AA is the floor, not the goal.

The five most-broken rules

Before you ship, check these.

If you catch one thing on this page, let it be this list. These are the issues we actually send back.

✓

Do

  • Use navy text on orange buttons — passes AA at 6.2:1.
  • One Market Pro moment per page — a tagline, a headline accent, or a quote mark.
  • Write in plain English. Replace every "utilize" with "use." Replace every "individual" with "person."
  • Give participants a compensation number up front. Never "up to" without a range.
  • Start from an existing preview file. Copy and modify; don't build from scratch.
✕

Don't

  • Never use white text on orange. Fails WCAG AA at 2.4:1. Still on our live marketing — we're fixing it.
  • Never set body copy in Market Pro. It's a decorative script, not a text font.
  • Never recolor the logo. Use crp-white.svg on dark backgrounds, full-color otherwise.
  • Never stretch or rotate the logo. Keep clear space = the height of "CRP".
  • Never promise medical outcomes. "May help" not "will help." Always "qualified participants" not "patients."
